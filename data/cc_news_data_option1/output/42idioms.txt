"Our brave troops have now been fighting in the Middle East for almost 19 years," he said in his 2019 State of the Union Address. "In Afghanistan and Iraq, nearly 7,000 American heroes have given their lives ... As a candidate for president, I loudly pledged a new approach. Great nations do not fight endless wars," he added.
 Trump cited the same approach when he addressed the nation regarding his decision to withdraw from Syria. "We're talking about sand and death," he said.
 So how did Trump, a leader that was so cautious with using military force, take such a bold step to kill Soleimani? Some experts are arguing that by taking that action, Trump avoided a broader confrontation between Iran and the US.
 "One way to stop sending Americans to Iraq to deal with existential threats like al-Qaeda and ISIS is to kill Qasem Soleimani," Mike Pregent, a senior fellow at Hudson Institute and former US intelligence officer in Iraq told The Jerusalem Post. "This is intelligence, strategically-driven decapitation of Qasem Soleimani's grip on Iraq. It's the only way Iraq has a chance. Iraqi protesters now have a chance to take their country back," he added.

"I don't think that's a loss on his base, and even the isolationists have to concede that eliminating Solemani was a major moment for the president."
 According to Schanzer, Trump's policy never approved Iran's activities, but in the past week, when the Iranians started to target US personnel directly, they left the US president no choice.
 "When Iran struck the Saudi oil fields, it was Saudi, not American," he said. "Even when Iran harassed ships on Hormuz, they were other countries - Naval vessels, not American.  He continued, "At the moment that Qasem Soleimani and his militias began to target the United States directly, that was when the light switch turned on for US strike," he continued. "And it became clear that Trump was not trying to deliver a modest message. He delivered a forceful one, about as forceful as one could imagine short of striking Iran itself."
 Schanzer emphasized that the answer to the question of what comes next relies entirely upon Iran's decision-making."They have a few choices," he said. "One is they could directly challenge the United States. This would be disastrous for the regime. According to some reporting, his advisers put the idea on a power point slide along with a number of other options, and he surprised them by choosing it."

In February 1966 I was asked by the editor of the Orrville Courier Crescent, to start writing a column "like the letters I used to send him when he was in college." He had been an intern at the paper the previous year before returning to Kent State University to finish his degree in journalism.  
 And that is how it all began. Never in my wildest dreams did I imagine that I would still be writing it almost 54 years later, outlasting numerous editors, and three weekly newspapers, along the way.  
 When I first started out the type was set by hand in the back of the Courier building. I loved the smell of the printer's ink. Now it is all electronic.  
 I don't know about you, but it has been very therapeutic for me. We have been through many good times - some bad times, and some sad times. You have gone along with me on my travels and adventures over the years.  
 I was born and raised in Holmes County, never out of Ohio until age 16 when the kids who finished their 4-H projects got to take a train ride to Pittsburgh. So, it was quite exciting to visit a lot of the United States and need a passport in my 30s to travel to places like Italy, Yugoslavia, Russia and Brazil.  
 I have appreciated the letters you sent, the face to face contact, and all those recipes you shared. I know you tried some of the dishes. One time I got a phone call from a reader who was making something and had a question about the ingredients. I was on an extension ladder painting the side of a building when the call came but I got down and looked up the recipe to answer her question.  
 You have suffered through child rearing with me from toddlers through college. When it all began Alan was almost 6, Valerie 4 and Robyn was a year and a half. You were there two years later when we learned Robyn had a brain tumor. It turned out to be benign, but she spent a month in the hospital in Cleveland. I stayed with her while Alan stayed with a neighbor and Valerie was farmed out to the grandparents.  
 You were there when the kids went off to college, got married and produced our soon-to-be 14 grandchildren. And now there are three great-grandchildren. It has required a lot of travel to other states over the years to keep in touch.  
 There was the period when Robyn and her family of six (ages 10 years down to seven months) moved in with us in Winesburg. We had invited them for two to three months while they were making the transition from Alabama back to Ohio. Through a series of circumstances, it turned into three years.  
 There were the deaths of our parents, and all the aunts and uncles. And now here we are at the top of the family heap - in the "back nine" of life.  
 There was a divorce and remarriage thus adding a stepson. And there were the moves from Ohio - first to South Carolina in August of 2013, following daughter Robyn and her family, and finally to North Carolina in 2018.  
 By my calculation that amounts to something like 2,802 columns. And that is enough. Now it's time to write letters to my children and grandchildren.  
 It has been my pleasure over these many decades.  
 Thanks for listening.
